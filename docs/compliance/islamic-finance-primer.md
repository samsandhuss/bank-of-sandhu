# Islamic Finance Primer (Demo Knowledge Base)

This document is an original, plain-language reference written for the Bank of
Sandhu prototype. It explains core Islamic finance concepts so the assistant
can answer customer questions in a grounded, citeable way. It is illustrative
and educational, not legal or Sharia advice.

## What makes a product Sharia-compliant

A Sharia-compliant product avoids interest (riba), excessive uncertainty
(gharar), and prohibited activities (haram). Instead of lending money at
interest, the bank typically buys, sells, or co-owns a real asset and earns a
disclosed profit or rental income. The economic outcome can resemble a
conventional product, but the contract structure and risk-sharing are
different, and the terms must be transparent to the customer.

## Murabaha (cost-plus sale)

Murabaha is a sale where the bank buys an asset the customer wants, then sells
it to the customer at the original cost plus an agreed, disclosed profit
margin. The customer repays in instalments. The key point is that the bank
takes ownership of the asset, even briefly, and the profit is fixed and known
up front rather than accruing as interest. Murabaha is commonly used for
vehicle and goods financing.

## Ijara (leasing)

Ijara is a lease. The bank owns an asset and leases it to the customer for
periodic rental payments. In a lease-to-own variant (Ijara wa Iqtina),
ownership transfers to the customer at the end of the term. Because the bank
owns the asset during the lease, it carries ownership risk, which is part of
what makes the structure compliant.

## Wakala (agency)

Wakala is an agency arrangement where the customer appoints the bank as agent
to manage funds or perform a service for a fee. It is often used in investment
accounts, where the bank invests on the customer's behalf and is paid an
agreed agency fee rather than guaranteed interest.

## Mudaraba and Musharaka (partnership)

Mudaraba is a profit-sharing partnership where one party provides capital and
the other provides expertise; profits are shared on a pre-agreed ratio while
financial losses fall on the capital provider. Musharaka is a joint venture
where all partners contribute capital and share profit and loss in proportion
to their stake. Both replace interest with genuine shared risk and reward.

## Riba (interest) and why it is avoided

Riba refers to a guaranteed, predetermined increase on a loan of money purely
for the passage of time. Islamic finance treats money as a medium of exchange
rather than a commodity that should itself generate more money. Profit is
considered legitimate when it is tied to real economic activity, asset
ownership, or risk-taking.

## Gharar (excessive uncertainty)

Gharar is avoidable, excessive uncertainty in a contract — for example, selling
something that does not yet exist or whose price is unknowable. Contracts
should clearly specify the asset, price, and terms so both parties understand
what they are agreeing to. Minor, unavoidable uncertainty is tolerated;
excessive speculation is not.

## Sharia governance and the Sharia board

A Sharia-compliant institution typically has a Sharia supervisory board of
qualified scholars who review products and issue rulings (fatawa) on whether a
structure is acceptable. Ongoing Sharia audit checks that day-to-day operations
match the approved structures. This governance layer is what allows customers
to trust that a product labelled Sharia-compliant genuinely is.

## Zakat

Zakat is an annual obligatory charitable contribution, generally calculated as
2.5% of qualifying net wealth held for a full lunar year (the Hawl). Eligible
assets typically include cash, certain investments, and gold above a minimum
threshold (nisab), with allowable liabilities deducted. Many Islamic banks
offer tools to help customers estimate Zakat, though the customer remains
responsible for their own calculation.

## Dual-mode banking (neutral and Islamic)

A dual-market bank can present the same underlying capability under two modes.
In neutral mode, products use conventional terminology (e.g. loan, APR). In
Islamic mode, the same need is met with a Sharia-compliant structure (e.g.
Murabaha or Ijara) and the terminology, disclosures, and profit/rental language
change accordingly. The goal is to serve different customer expectations
without misrepresenting either product.

## Anti-money-laundering and customer due diligence

Regardless of mode, regulated banks must verify customer identity and screen
against sanctions and politically-exposed-person lists before opening accounts.
This obligation comes from financial-crime regulation rather than Sharia, but
applies equally to Islamic products. Customers should expect identity checks
as a standard, lawful part of onboarding.

## Scope and disclaimer

This primer is a simplified educational summary created for a portfolio
prototype. Real Sharia rulings vary between scholars and jurisdictions, and
nothing here should be relied on as legal, financial, or religious advice.
